# Surface waves and dispersion

Rawlinson, N. (2007). *Surface waves and dispersion*. Lecture 5 of PEAT8002 — Seismology, Research School of Earth Sciences, Australian National University.

## Main message

Surface waves dominate long-period seismograms because they decay less rapidly with distance than body waves. This lecture derives Rayleigh and Love waves in simple media, defines dispersion, and links phase and group velocity.

## Rayleigh waves

- Surface waves arise from the interaction of elastic waves with the free surface.
- They are composed of coupled P and SV waves whose amplitude decays with depth.
- On a homogeneous half-space the Rayleigh phase velocity is proportional to the shear-wave velocity and independent of frequency.
- The particle motion is retrograde elliptical at the surface.

## Love waves

- Love waves are made of SH motion trapped near the surface.
- They require a velocity structure that varies with depth; they cannot exist in a uniform half-space.
- The simplest model is a layer over a half-space with a higher S-wave velocity.
- The dispersion relation gives a finite number of discrete modes at each frequency; the lowest-velocity mode is the fundamental mode.

## Dispersion

- In heterogeneous media, phase velocity becomes frequency-dependent.
- Love waves are dispersive; long-period surface-wave arrivals can produce a long wave train.
- Group velocity is the speed of the energy envelope, related to phase velocity by U = dω/dk.
