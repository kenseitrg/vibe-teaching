# Surface Waves and Free Oscillations

Igel, H. (2007). *Surface Waves and Free Oscillations*. Seismology and the Earth's Deep Interior course notes, SEDI.

## Main message

These course notes introduce Rayleigh and Love waves from first principles, derive their dispersion in simple layered media, and connect surface waves to the free oscillations of the Earth.

## Rayleigh waves

- A post-critically reflected SV wave and an evanescent P wave propagate together along the free surface and decay with depth.
- Their coupled P-SV motion satisfies the stress-free surface boundary condition.
- For a Poisson solid, the Rayleigh phase velocity is about 0.92 times the shear-wave velocity.
- Particle motion is retrograde elliptical near the surface and becomes prograde at depth.
- Rayleigh waves on a homogeneous half-space are non-dispersive.

## Love waves

- SH waves do not exist as surface waves on a homogeneous half-space.
- In a low-velocity layer over a higher-velocity half-space, multiple reflections interfere constructively and trap energy near the surface.
- The resulting dispersion relation is frequency-dependent; only discrete modes exist at each frequency.
- The fundamental mode has the lowest phase velocity; higher modes appear above cut-off frequencies.

## Dispersion, wave packets, and group velocity

- In a two-layer structure, phase velocity varies with frequency.
- The sum of two nearby frequencies produces a carrier wave (phase velocity) and an envelope (group velocity).
- Group velocity is the speed at which energy propagates; it can differ from phase velocity.

## Free oscillations

- Very large earthquakes excite standing waves (normal modes) of the whole Earth.
- Spheroidal modes are analogous to P-SV surface waves; toroidal modes are analogous to SH (Love) waves.
- The eigenfrequencies are affected by Earth's rotation and lateral heterogeneity.

## Relation to lecture notes

These notes are useful for the physical-intuition part of the lecture: why Rayleigh waves exist, why Love waves require a low-velocity layer, and the difference between phase and group velocity.
