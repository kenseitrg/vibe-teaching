Any discontinuity in the data results in the scattering of seismic energy, creating “diffractions”.  The diffracting point acts as a secondary point source;  energy propagating as wavefronts back to each receiver at the surface.

Diffractions will occur at any discontinuity.  It could be an abrupt change like a fault or a larger scale feature like a channel or syncline feature or the opposite such as a salt dome.

What we know as Huygens’ principle, stated that each point on the wavefront can be regarded as the source of a subsequent wave. Destructive interference destroys the subsequent waves except along the common tangent producing a new wavefront.

Attenuation
As well as the kinematics of the waves we must also understand what governs attenuation of our wavefront. We usually measure P-wave amplitude. Just for a simple 1d model, consider what affects the attenuation of the P-wave energy that we record?
Attenuation
  1. Reduction in amplitude or energy caused by the transmitting media or system. Usually includes geometric divergence effects as waves spread out from a source as well as conversion of energy into heat (absorption) and other factors affecting amplitude, such as transmissivity losses and mode conversion.  

[Encyclopedic Dictionary of Applied Geophysics,  Robert E. Sheriff 4th Edition. ]
Like seismic velocity, attenuation is frequency dependent. Some mechanisms such as absorption more than others.  The frequency dependent nature of phase and amplitude relative to a reference frequency is dealt with by our Q amplitude and phase corrections. 

Question: Which of these attenuation effects do we correct for?  How good a job do we do?  How may the corrections we apply effect certain processing steps?
What is the difference between amplitude recovery and spherical divergence correction?

Some properties of shot records/gathers
123
Raw Shots will contain all types of data; primary; multiple; diffractions; noise; refractions, all of which have different characteristics.
Shot points mix traces with different offsets, so the shot points present “Normal moveout” (the increase in reflection arrival time from having travelled the extra distance due to the longer offset).
Also as the offset increases and as we get to further reflection angles we can also start to see the data in the shot domain refracting, diving waves and even mode converted events.
And also mix traces with different subsurface reflection points,  so the recorded shot gather also presents moveout (differences in reflection arrival time) due to different offsets being reflected from different parts of the subsurface. Thus the recorded seismic shot can also show us information on the structure.
So taking out the travel time due to NMO (apply an NMO correction), although we have not correctly positioned the data,  we will start to see the structure, although the far offsets may appear to be stretched due to the NMO correction.
Thus if we are to apply shot domain processing (FK filter, Linear noise attenuation etc..) it is essential we recognise what the different events in shot gather are.   Pre prepared to answer questions form you supervisor’s as to which events in the shot domain match up with which events on the stack section.
